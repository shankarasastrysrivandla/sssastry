Types of Solutions & Concentration Expressing

Classification of Solutions

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. Subdivided based on physical states of solute and solvent:

Solute Solvent Common NCERT Example
Gas Gas Air (Oxygen + Nitrogen mixture)
Liquid Liquid Ethanol dissolved in Water
Solid Liquid Glucose or NaCl dissolved in Water
Gas Solid Solution of Hydrogen in Palladium (\(\text{H}_2/\text{Pd}\))
Liquid Solid Amalgam of Mercury with Sodium (\(\text{Hg}-\text{Na}\))
Solid Solid Copper dissolved in Gold (Alloys)

Expressing Concentration

Quantitative measurement of solute dissolved in solvent:

  • Molarity (\(M\)): Moles of solute per litre of solution. Temperature dependent.
    \(M = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{Volume of solution in L}}\)
  • Molality (\(m\)): Moles of solute per kg of solvent. Temperature independent.
    \(m = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{Mass of solvent in kg}}\)
  • Mole Fraction (\(\chi_A\)): Ratio of moles of component A to total moles.
    \(\chi_A = \frac{n_A}{n_A + n_B}\quad (\chi_A + \chi_B = 1)\)
  • Parts per Million (ppm): Used for trace quantities.
    \(\text{ppm} = \frac{\text{Mass of solute}}{\text{Total mass of solution}} \times 10^6\)

Solubility of Gases & Solids

Factors Influencing Solubility

Solids in Liquids: Follows "like dissolves like". Polar solutes (NaCl) dissolve in polar solvents (water). Dissolution is endothermic (\(\Delta H > 0\)), solubility increases with temperature. If exothermic (\(\Delta H < 0\)), solubility decreases. Pressure has no significant effect due to solids/liquids being highly incompressible.

Gases in Liquids: Exothermic dissolution (\(\Delta H < 0\)). Solubility **decreases** as temperature increases. Solubility increases significantly with pressure.

Henry's Law

At a constant temperature, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas present above the surface of the liquid or solution.

\(p = K_H \cdot \chi\)

Where \(p\) is partial pressure, \(\chi\) is mole fraction of gas in solution, and \(K_H\) is the Henry's Law constant.

  • Higher \(K_H\) at a given pressure means **lower solubility** of gas.
  • \(K_H\) increases with temperature, which is why aquatic species are more comfortable in cold water than warm water.
  • Applications: Carbonated drinks (sealed under high pressure), Scuba diving (avoiding Bends by diluting air with Helium), Anoxia at high altitudes.

Vapor Pressure & Raoult's Law

Vapor Pressure of Liquid-Liquid Solutions

In a volatile binary solution of components A and B, both molecules evaporate, reaching equilibrium. According to Raoult's Law, the partial vapor pressure of each volatile component is directly proportional to its mole fraction in solution.

\(p_A = p_A^0 \cdot \chi_A \quad \text{and} \quad p_B = p_B^0 \cdot \chi_B\)

Total pressure \(P_{\text{total}}\) over the solution is:

\(P_{\text{total}} = p_A + p_B = p_A^0 \chi_A + p_B^0 (1 - \chi_A)\)

where \(p_A^0\) and \(p_B^0\) are the vapor pressures of the pure components A and B.

Vapor Pressure of Solid-in-Liquid Solutions

When a non-volatile solute (e.g., Glucose, NaCl) is added to a solvent, solute particles occupy surface sites. This reduces the number of solvent molecules escaping into vapor phase.

  • Vapor pressure of a solution containing non-volatile solute is **always lower** than pure solvent.
  • The lowering depends only on the **concentration** of solute particles, not their nature.
  • Mathematically: \(P_{\text{solution}} = p_{\text{solvent}}^0 \cdot \chi_{\text{solvent}}\).

Ideal and Non-Ideal Solutions & Azeotropes

Ideal Solutions

Obey Raoult's law over the entire range of concentration. Formed when adhesive molecular forces (A-B) are equal to cohesive forces (A-A and B-B).

  • \(\Delta H_{\text{mix}} = 0\) (No heat absorbed or released)
  • \(\Delta V_{\text{mix}} = 0\) (No volume expansion or contraction)
  • Examples: n-hexane + n-heptane, Bromoethane + Chloroethane, Benzene + Toluene.

Non-Ideal Solutions

Do not obey Raoult's law. Divided into two classes:

  1. Positive Deviation: A-B interactions are weaker than A-A/B-B interactions. Escaping tendency increases, so \(P_{\text{total}} > P_{\text{ideal}}\). \(\Delta H_{\text{mix}} > 0, \Delta V_{\text{mix}} > 0\).
    Example: Ethanol + Acetone, Carbon disulfide + Acetone.
  2. Negative Deviation: A-B interactions are stronger than A-A/B-B (e.g. hydrogen bonding). Escaping tendency decreases, so \(P_{\text{total}} < P_{\text{ideal}}\). \(\Delta H_{\text{mix}} < 0, \Delta V_{\text{mix}} < 0\).
    Example: Chloroform + Acetone, Nitric acid + Water.

Azeotropes: Binary mixtures having same composition in liquid & vapor phase, boiling at a constant temp. Positive deviation forms minimum boiling azeotrope; negative deviation forms maximum boiling azeotrope.

Colligative Properties & Molar Mass

The Four Properties

Properties of solutions that depend solely on the number of solute particles relative to solvent molecules, independent of their chemical identity:

  • Relative Lowering of Vapor Pressure:
    \(\frac{p_1^0 - p_1}{p_1^0} = \chi_2 = \frac{n_2}{n_1 + n_2}\)
  • Elevation of Boiling Point (\(\Delta T_b\)): Solute addition decreases vapor pressure, meaning higher temperature is needed to make vapor pressure equal atmospheric pressure.
    \(\Delta T_b = T_b - T_b^0 = K_b \cdot m\)
    \(K_b\) is Ebullioscopic constant (Molal boiling point elevation constant).
  • Depression of Freezing Point (\(\Delta T_f\)): At freezing point, solid and liquid phases have same vapor pressure. Vapor pressure lowering pushes freezing point lower.
    \(\Delta T_f = T_f^0 - T_f = K_f \cdot m\)
    \(K_f\) is Cryoscopic constant (Molal freezing point depression constant).

Osmotic Pressure (\(\Pi\))

Osmosis is the spontaneous flow of solvent molecules through a semi-permeable membrane (SPM) from pure solvent to solution. Osmotic pressure (\(\Pi\)) is the excess pressure applied on solution side to stop osmosis.

\(\Pi = C \cdot R \cdot T\)

Where \(C\) is molarity, \(R\) is gas constant, and \(T\) is temperature in Kelvin.

  • Reverse Osmosis (RO): If pressure applied exceeds osmotic pressure (\(P > \Pi\)), solvent flows from solution to pure solvent. Used in desalination.
  • RBC behaviors: Hypertonic (solution concentration > 0.9% NaCl, cells shrink), Hypotonic (concentration < 0.9%, cells swell/burst), Isotonic (equal concentration).

Abnormal Molar Masses & van 't Hoff Factor (i)

Abnormal Molar Mass

Colligative properties are inversely proportional to solute molar mass. When solutes undergo dissociation (e.g. ionic salts) or association (e.g. organic acids in non-polar solvents), the number of particles in solution changes, leading to anomalous calculated molar masses.

To account for this, J.H. van 't Hoff introduced the factor \(i\):

\(i = \frac{\text{Observed colligative property}}{\text{Calculated colligative property}} = \frac{\text{Normal molar mass}}{\text{Abnormal molar mass}}\)
\(i = \frac{\text{Total moles of particles after association/dissociation}}{\text{Total moles of particles before association/dissociation}}\)

Calculations & Modified Equations

  • For Dissociation (e.g., \(\text{NaCl} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+ + \text{Cl}^-\)): Particles increase, so \(i > 1\).
    \(i = 1 + (n - 1)\alpha\)
    where \(\alpha\) is degree of dissociation, \(n\) is number of ions formed. For strong electrolytes with complete dissociation, \(i = n\) (e.g., \(i=2\) for \(\text{NaCl}\), \(i=3\) for \(\text{K}_2\text{SO}_4\)).
  • For Association (e.g., dimers of acetic acid in benzene): Particles decrease, so \(i < 1\).
    \(i = 1 - (1 - \frac{1}{n})\beta\)
    where \(\beta\) is degree of association. For dimerizing acids with complete association, \(i = 0.5\).

Modified Colligative Equations:

\(\frac{\Delta p}{p^0} = i \chi_2 \quad | \quad \Delta T_b = i K_b m \quad | \quad \Delta T_f = i K_f m \quad | \quad \Pi = i C R T\)
