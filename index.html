Werner's Theory & Double Salts

Double Salts vs Coordination Compounds

Double Salts (e.g., Mohr's Salt [FeSO4·(NH4)2SO4·6H2O], Carnallite) dissociate completely into simple ions when dissolved in water. They lose their identity in solution.

Coordination Compounds (e.g., K4[Fe(CN)6]) do not dissociate completely. The complex ion [Fe(CN)6]4- remains intact and does not give tests for individual Fe2+ or CN- ions.

Werner's Postulates

  • Primary Valency: Ionizable, corresponds to the oxidation state of the metal. Satisfied by negative ions. Represented by dotted lines.
  • Secondary Valency: Non-ionizable, corresponds to the coordination number (fixed for a metal). Satisfied by neutral molecules or negative ions. Represented by thick solid lines.
Example: CoCl3·6NH3 forms [Co(NH3)6]Cl3. When treated with AgNO3, 3 moles of AgCl precipitate, meaning 3 Cl- ions are outside the coordination sphere (primary valencies).

Nomenclature Rules (IUPAC)

Key Rules for Naming

  1. Order of Naming: Cation is named first, followed by the anion, regardless of whether they are complex or simple.
  2. Naming Coordination Sphere: Ligands are named first in alphabetical order, followed by the central metal atom.
  3. Ligand Prefixes: Use di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-, hexa- for simple ligands. Use bis-, tris-, tetrakis- for complex ligands containing prefixes (e.g., ethylenediamine).
  4. Ligand Names: Anionic ligands end in -o (e.g., chloride → chlorido, cyanide → cyanido). Neutral ligands use special names (e.g., H2O → aqua, NH3 → ammine, CO → carbonyl).

Naming the Central Metal

  • If the coordination entity is a cation or neutral, the metal is named as is (e.g., Cobalt, Platinum) followed by its oxidation state in Roman numerals in parentheses.
  • If the coordination entity is an anion, the metal name ends in -ate (e.g., Cobalt → cobaltate, Iron → ferrate, Copper → cuprate, Silver → argentate).
Examples:
• [Co(NH3)6]Cl3 → Hexaamminecobalt(III) chloride
• K4[Fe(CN)6] → Potassium hexacyanidoferrate(II)

Isomerism in Coordination Compounds

Structural Isomerism

  • Linkage Isomerism: Occurs in complexes with ambidentate ligands (e.g., [Co(NH3)5(NO2)]Cl2 [nitro, red] vs [Co(NH3)5(ONO)]Cl2 [nitrito, yellow]).
  • Coordination Isomerism: Interchange of ligands between cationic and anionic entities (e.g., [Co(NH3)6][Cr(CN)6] and [Cr(NH3)6][Co(CN)6]).
  • Ionisation Isomerism: Gives different ions in solution (e.g., [Co(NH3)5SO4]Br [red-violet, gives BaSO4 negative, AgBr positive] vs [Co(NH3)5Br]SO4 [red, gives BaSO4 precipitate]).
  • Solvate / Hydrate Isomerism: Differs by whether water is inside or outside the coordination sphere (e.g., [Cr(H2O)6]Cl3 [violet] vs [Cr(H2O)5Cl]Cl2·H2O [grey-green]).

Stereoisomerism

Geometrical Isomerism: Common in coordination numbers 4 (square planar only, e.g. cis/trans [Pt(NH3)2Cl2]) and 6 (octahedral, e.g. cis/trans [Co(NH3)4Cl2]+, and fac/mer [Co(NH3)3(NO2)3]). Tetrahedral complexes do not show geometrical isomerism due to relative positions being adjacent.

Optical Isomerism: Non-superimposable mirror images (chiral). Common in octahedral complexes with bidentate ligands (e.g., [Co(en)3]3+ or cis-[Co(en)2Cl2]+). The trans isomer is symmetrical (has plane of symmetry) and optically inactive.

Valence Bond Theory (VBT)

Core Assumptions

VBT explains bonding in terms of hybridization of metal orbitals. The metal provides empty orbitals to accept lone pairs from ligands.

C.N. Geometry Hybridization Examples
4 Tetrahedral sp3 [Ni(CO)4], [Ni(Cl)4]2-
4 Square Planar dsp2 [Ni(CN)4]2-, [Pt(Cl)4]2-
6 Octahedral (Inner) d2sp3 [Fe(CN)6]4-, [Co(NH3)6]3+
6 Octahedral (Outer) sp3d2 [CoF6]3-, [Fe(H2O)6]2+

Inner vs. Outer Orbital Complexes

  • Inner Orbital Complexes (Low Spin): Formed when strong field ligands force d-electrons to pair up, leaving inner (n-1)d orbitals free for hybridization (e.g., d2sp3). Usually diamagnetic or low spin.
  • Outer Orbital Complexes (High Spin): Formed when weak field ligands cannot force pairing. Outer nd orbitals are used for hybridization (e.g., sp3d2). Usually paramagnetic or high spin.
Magnetic Moment (μ): Calculated using the spin-only formula: μ = √[n(n+2)] Bohr Magnetons (BM), where n = number of unpaired electrons.

Crystal Field Theory (CFT)

The Electrostatic Model

CFT treats the metal-ligand bond as purely ionic, resulting from electrostatic attractions. The approach of negative ligand point charges splits the degenerate d-orbitals of the metal ion into sets with different energies.

Octahedral Field Splitting (Δo)

Orbitals pointing along the axes (dx2-y2, dz2) experience more repulsion and rise in energy to form the eg set. Orbitals pointing between the axes (dxy, dyz, dxz) experience less repulsion and fall in energy to form the t2g set. The gap is Δo.

Tetrahedral Field Splitting (Δt)

The splitting is inverted: t2 (higher) and e (lower). The splitting energy is smaller: Δt = (4/9) Δo. Thus, tetrahedral complexes are almost always high spin.

Spectrochemical Series & Color

Ligands are arranged in order of increasing field strength:

I- < Br- < S2- < SCN- < Cl- < F- < OH- < C2O42- < H2O < NCS- < EDTA4- < NH3 < en < CN- < CO
  • Strong Field (e.g., CN-, CO): Δo > P (pairing energy). Electrons pair in t2g before entering eg (low spin).
  • Weak Field (e.g., F-, Cl-): Δo < P. Electrons fill orbitals singly before pairing (high spin).

Color: Light absorption promotes an electron from t2g to eg (d-d transition). The observed color is complementary to the absorbed wavelength.

Metal Carbonyls & Applications

Bonding in Metal Carbonyls

Metal carbonyls (e.g., [Ni(CO)4], [Fe(CO)5], [Cr(CO)6]) feature synergic bonding:

  1. σ Bond: Ligand CO donates its lone pair of electrons from carbon to an empty d-orbital of the metal.
  2. π Back-donation: Filled d-orbital of the metal back-donates electrons into the empty anti-bonding π* molecular orbital of CO.

This feedback mechanism strengthens both the M-C bond and weakens the C-O bond, providing high stability to carbonyls.

Applications of Coordination Compounds

  • In Medicine: Cisplatin (cis-[Pt(NH3)2Cl2]) is used in cancer chemotherapy. EDTA is used in treatment of lead poisoning.
  • In Biology: Chlorophyll contains Mg2+, Hemoglobin contains Fe2+, and Vitamin B12 contains Co3+.
  • In Metallurgy: Extraction of silver and gold involves forming soluble cyano-complexes (e.g., [Au(CN)2]-).
  • In Qualitative Analysis: Detection of Ni2+ using dimethylglyoxime (DMG) forming a rosy red complex.
